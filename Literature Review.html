Introduction

The literature linking economic inequality to intergenerational mobility and social cohesion in developing nations is large, methodologically diverse, and conditional in its conclusions. Across theory, cross-country comparison, and quasi-experimental micro work, higher inequality is broadly associated with lower mobility and weaker cohesion, but the effect is rarely mechanical. It depends on which inequality is measured, the channels through which advantage is transmitted, and the institutional setting that mediates the relationship. The strongest evidence comes from studies that trace specific mechanisms — schooling, credit, labour markets, discrimination — to concrete outcomes for the children of the poor. Broader cross-country regressions, by contrast, often show associations whose magnitude is sensitive to specification and whose causal interpretation is contested.

This review synthesises full-text evidence as its core base, with abstract-only studies used as supplementary support and interpreted cautiously. The reviewed work spans the measurement of inequality and mobility, human-capital formation, credit and insurance, labour-market structure, spatial opportunity, group-based exclusion, trust and institutions, redistribution, and regional heterogeneity. A central finding is that "inequality" is not a single treatment: its consequences differ by whether one studies income, wealth, or opportunity, and by whether the binding constraint is credit, school quality, discrimination, or geography. The evidence therefore supports a restrained conclusion — inequality tends to depress mobility and cohesion under identifiable conditions, but the size and even the sign of the effect shift with the surrounding institutional context.

Measuring inequality, mobility, and cohesion

Conclusions hinge on what is measured and how.

Concepts and their consequences

Income inequality, wealth inequality, and inequality of opportunity behave differently, and the choice matters for findings. Corak (2013) documents the "Great Gatsby" pattern — more unequal societies show lower intergenerational income mobility — and shows it holds across many countries, including developing ones. Roemer and Trannoy (2016) argue that inequality of opportunity, which strips out differences attributable to effort, is the more normatively relevant object, while Brunori, Ferreira, and Peragine (2013) show that opportunity inequality is especially high in many developing economies and correlates with low mobility.

Mobility itself is captured through income elasticities, educational persistence, or occupational transitions, each telling a slightly different story. Narayan et al. (2018) find that educational persistence is steeper than income persistence in much of the Global South, precisely where school quality varies sharply by family background. Measurement error compounds the difficulty: Mazumder (2005) shows that short or noisy earnings windows attenuate estimated persistence, biasing mobility upward.

Cohesion as an outcome

Cohesion is harder to operationalise. Researchers proxy it with generalised trust, civic participation, and perceptions of fairness. Alesina and La Ferrara (2002) find that inequality lowers trust, but the relationship is sensitive to whether respondents attribute outcomes to effort or to circumstance — a distinction that itself varies with how visible and rigid social hierarchies are.

Education and human-capital transmission

The most consistently identified channel from inequality to immobility.

Access versus quality

Where school quality, early-childhood inputs, and tertiary access track family income, the children of poorer households accumulate less human capital regardless of ability. Hanushek and Woessmann (2012) show that learning — not years of schooling — drives the growth and mobility payoff, which reframes access-based policy. Quasi-experimental evaluations of school expansion and scholarships (Duflo, 2001; Muralidharan and Sundararaman, 2015) find meaningful gains in enrolment and attainment, especially for girls and rural children, but the returns are muted where teaching quality is low or credentials are diluted.

Heckman and Mosso (2014) stress the dynamic complementarity of investments: early gaps in cognitive and non-cognitive skills widen without sustained follow-up, so one-off interventions rarely close the mobility gap. This helps reconcile mixed programme results — effects depend on whether investment is maintained across the life cycle.

Credit constraints and household investment

Poor households underinvest in children even when returns are high.

A recurring mechanism is financial: families cannot borrow against their children's future earnings, so they underinvest in education, health, and migration. Galor and Zeira (1993) formalise how credit constraints under inequality produce persistent underinvestment and low mobility. Empirically, Bossuroy et al. (2022) show in a large multi-country trial that relaxing capital and psychosocial constraints together raises investment and consumption among the very poor, with effects persisting beyond the intervention.

Yet credit access alone is rarely sufficient. Banerjee et al. (2015), in a six-country evaluation of microcredit, find modest and uneven effects on the outcomes that matter for mobility. Where returns are uncertain or risk is uninsured, families remain cautious; the literature increasingly links mobility to the wider architecture of insurance and asset-building rather than credit in isolation.

Labour markets, informality, and returns to skill

Where human capital is priced — and where its returns are blunted.

Large informal sectors, segmented markets, and weak enforcement compress the returns to education for those without networks or formal entry points. Fields (2011) documents how labour-market segmentation in developing economies decouples schooling from earnings, weakening the schooling-to-mobility link. La Porta and Shleifer (2014) show that informality persists structurally and that informal firms remain small and unproductive, limiting the absorption of educated entrants from poor backgrounds.

The effect is uneven across settings. Where formal employment is accessible and wage-setting rewards measured skill rather than family background, mobility is higher; where hiring is rigid or dominated by a few employers, even well-educated entrants face barriers. Formalisation and product-market competition emerge as complements to schooling, not substitutes for it.

Spatial inequality and the geography of opportunity

Where a child grows up shapes their prospects.

Opportunity is unevenly distributed in space. Chetty et al. (2014), though focused on the United States, established the methodological template now applied across the Global South: mobility varies markedly across regions and neighbourhoods within the same country. Studies of internal migration (Bryan, Chowdhury, and Mobarak, 2014) find that enabling moves to higher-opportunity areas can raise incomes substantially, but moving costs, informal tenure, and social ties limit who relocates.

Spatial inequality interacts with public goods: where local financing of schools and clinics tracks local income, geography entrenches disadvantage across generations. Effects therefore reflect not only where families live but how opportunity and public investment are distributed across places.

Group-based inequality

Horizontal inequalities compound economic disadvantage and corrode cohesion.

Inequality is rarely only about income. Gender, ethnicity, caste, and region structure opportunity in ways that lower mobility independently of household income. Munshi (2019) shows how caste networks shape labour-market entry in India, simultaneously providing insurance and constraining mobility out of traditional occupations. Stewart (2016) argues that horizontal inequalities between groups are especially corrosive of social cohesion — often more than vertical income gaps — because they align grievance with identity.

Interventions aimed at excluded groups, from girls' schooling to anti-discrimination measures, can raise both mobility and perceived fairness, but the gains depend on enforcement and prevailing norms. Where norms are rigid or institutions weak, formal equality of access does not close gaps.

Social cohesion, trust, and institutions

A feedback loop between inequality, trust, and the capacity to act collectively.

High and visible inequality erodes generalised trust, weakens collective action, and can fuel grievance, especially where it aligns with group identity. Putnam (2007) and subsequent work link diversity and inequality to lower social capital in the short run, while emphasising that institutions mediate the relationship. Lower cohesion in turn undermines the provision of public goods and the political support for mobility-enhancing policy, creating a self-reinforcing loop.

Perceptions matter as much as measured inequality. Alesina, Stantcheva, and Teso (2018) show that beliefs about mobility — not just its actual level — shape preferences for redistribution, and that these beliefs are systematically biased. Where the state is seen as fair and capable, the same level of inequality is less corrosive of trust.

Redistribution and social protection

Policy can break the transmission — under conditions.

Conditional and unconditional transfers, social insurance, and public schooling can weaken the inequality–mobility link when they reach the constrained and are sustained. Fiszbein and Schady (2009), reviewing conditional cash transfers, find robust gains in school enrolment and health use, though effects on learning and long-run mobility are more variable. Programmes that pair income support with investment in children's human capital tend to outperform pure transfers on mobility outcomes.

Unfunded or poorly targeted policies fare worse. Where administration is weak, leakage high, or benefits captured by the better-off, redistribution may shift resources without changing the underlying opportunity structure. The literature increasingly judges policy within the wider fiscal and institutional constraint rather than in isolation.

Heterogeneity across developing regions

The same inequality maps onto different outcomes by context.

Effects are stronger where schooling quality varies sharply by background, where labour markets are segmented, and where group-based barriers are entrenched; they are weaker where access to quality public services is broad. Narayan et al. (2018) document distinct patterns across Latin American, Sub-Saharan African, and South Asian settings, reflecting their histories of land, education, and labour institutions. This heterogeneity cautions against universal claims: results from one institutional setting often do not transfer cleanly to another, and the binding constraint differs across contexts.

Methodological approaches and evidence quality

Why estimates vary, and how much weight they bear.

The literature is methodologically diverse, which helps explain divergent findings. Intergenerational elasticities are sensitive to how income is measured and over what horizon (Mazumder, 2005). Cross-country comparisons face endogeneity and comparability problems, while quasi-experimental studies offer cleaner identification of specific channels but capture partial-equilibrium effects that may not aggregate. Trust and cohesion measures rely on surveys whose framing shapes responses.

Taken together, the evidence supports the general claim that inequality dampens mobility and cohesion, but not a single universal magnitude. The most credible estimates are mechanism-specific and context-bound; sweeping cross-country coefficients should be read as suggestive rather than decisive.

Synthesis and conclusion

Rising inequality tends to lower intergenerational mobility and weaken social cohesion in developing nations, but the effect is conditional rather than automatic. It is strongest where inequality is transmitted through unequal schooling, binding credit constraints, segmented labour markets, and group-based exclusion, and where weak or captured institutions fail to widen access to opportunity. Where states are fair and capable and public services reach the constrained, the link is substantially weaker.

The most useful question is therefore not whether inequality harms mobility and cohesion in general, but which inequalities, transmitted through which channels, and under which institutions, are most damaging — and which policies most reliably break the transmission. The evidence points to combinations: income support paired with sustained investment in children's human capital, formalisation alongside competition, and redistribution backed by administrative capacity. Single instruments, applied without attention to context, rarely move the needle.
